Dravidian Linguistics I

“To Sit” in South Dravidian

This past week, I was making my way through the Dēvīmahātme, a text in Old Tulu purported to have been composed in the 13th century ce. I’ve found many idiosyncratic verb forms in Old Tulu, but this one from the Dēvīmahātme caught my eye: «ōlakā» “to sit,” used in the following context (90:7.1), alongside many others:

vimānoṁ-ṭə ə-pp-a ōlakā-ζə-na-ārə
aerial chariot-loc to be-npst-inf to sit-pfv-agnz-3sgf.hon

Not only is this an unusual base shape, but it is also reminiscent of the familiar Tamil «uṭkār» “id.” Naturally, this piqued my interest, and I wanted to delve further.

In the Madras Tamil Lexicon, one finds «uṭkār» (iv) to be listed as ‘colloquial’ usage, and its effective form «uṭkārttu» (iii) as ‘local’ usage. From these entries, the reader is directed toward «uḷukkā» (xi), which is apparently a western form. One also finds a variant form «uḷukkāru» (class unknown). Perhaps the forms in ‹uḷu-› can be seen as original, since ‹ṭ› is a regular sandhi variant of ‹ṭ› before a geminate plosive when an intervening vowel drops out. To make matters more strange, the Dravidian Etymological Dictionary never once lists any of these forms.

In modern spoken Tamil, we normally hear «okkārɯ» as the word of choice for “to sit.” It is regular, following the pattern of other disyllabic affective verbs ending in ‹r›: a past in «okkā-nd-», a present in «okkār(ɯ)-r-», and a future in «okkāru-v-». However, in my mother’s own dialect, I hear more idiosyncratic forms which emerge in verb conjugation. I commonly hear her say «okkāṇḍɯ» for «okkāndɯ» “having sat,̦” and «okkāččɯ-iṇḍɯ» for «okkāndɯ-giṭṭɯ» “having s[e]at[ed oneself].” The former warrants a stem in *«uḷukkāḷ»
