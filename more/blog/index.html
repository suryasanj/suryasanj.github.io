Background

Tamil has an idiosyncratic verbal system, which is unusually complex amongst Dravidian languages. Verbs are classified based on past and future stem, with the present stem predictably following the future stem. Karl F. Graul’s seven-class system is the most commonly used scheme, adapted into the Madras Tamil Lexicon as a thirteen-class scheme. Leigh (1951), Emeneau (1967), and Agesthialingom (1971) independently

Rationale

There are two main, conceivable purposes of using a verb class system in Tamil—one pedagogical and one comparative. The pedagogical rationale is to ensure that the learner is able to quickly pick up patterns and become fluent; here, reduction is perhaps more to prevent overwhelming the learner (say, with thirteen classes) than to isolate underlying morphemes. On the other hand, the comparative rationale is to group verbs that use the same medials to allow easier comparison with systems in related languages.

Graul’s classes function well for pedagogy, with the exception of Class I, which may confuse learners early on. Leigh (1951), Emeneau (1967), and Agesthialingom (1971) independently proposed their own schemes to better facilitate comparison.

The New Proposal

Reduction through Morphophonemics

Weak Verbs with Lateral-final Bases

I start with Graul’s (1855) and Ramaswami Aiyar’s (1938) contention that the following morphophonemic rule operates in Tamil at morpheme boundaries where the first morpheme is a verbal base and the second is a bound morpheme:

$$ \left\{ \begin{matrix} l\\ ḷ\\ \end{matrix} \right\} \rightarrow \left\{ \begin{matrix} ṉ\\ ṇ\\ \end{matrix} \right\} {\Large /} \#\_t\# $$

Although this rule tracks in Class I verbs with the past-tense suffix «-t-» and the verbal noun formatives «-ti» and «-tal» (e. g., «koṇ-ṭ-», «koṇṭi», and «koṇṭal» for «koḷ» “to seize”), it falls short with the first-person nonpast portmanteau suffixes «-tɯ» and «-tum» (e. g., «kōṭɯ» “I seize” and «kōṭum» “we seize”). Worse yet, the latter is seen in opposition to rule-abiding «koṇṭum» “we seized,” which apparently attests the same underlying phonological structure («koḷ-t-um» vs. «koḷ-tum»).

To account for this, as well as the apparently dubious lateral nasalization (untriggered by a surrounding nasal), Leigh, Emeneau, and Agesthialingom propose that these verbs take «-nt-» instead. As such, the portmanteau morph examples could be resegmented as «koḷ-nt-um» (= «koṇṭum») and «koḷ-tum» («kōṭum»), explaining the varied surface forms.

Lateral Nasalization

Lateral nasalization seemingly also occurs in the case of Tamil appellative verbs which end with a final lateral. Consider the following paradigm, only showing the third-person forms:

3pln nall-a uḷ(ḷ)-a al(l)-a

In the 3sgn forms, the lateral is replaced by a nasal. There are three conditions that may allow for this to happen: (1) the 3sgn suffix is alone in this paradigm in being preceded by another suffix «-n-»; (2) the 3sgn suffix here is «-ntɯ» instead of expected *«-tɯ»; or (3) lateral nasalization must be admitted.

(2) is out of the question, as such a suffix appears in no other context. The 3sgn is always «-(t)tɯ» in appellatives. (1) is possible, but it seems unlikely, since we see it nowhere else in the appellative system. The Tirumurukāṯṯuppaṭai does attest a form «naṉṉar» “goodness” (v. 64), but this may be in analogy to «muṉṉar», given «naṉ-» appears quite frequently as a combining form (before nasals). It’s also unclear what this «-n-» suffix may be: is it a genitive, an agentive, or a pleonastically-added noun formative? As it precedes a suffix and not a free morpheme (i. e., what we would consider a demonstrative pronoun like «atɯ» “that”), it cannot be a relational or case suffix, so that precludes the genitive and agentive hypotheses. It may be a noun formative, but we have no attestations for *«naṉ», *«uṇ», and *«aṉ», respectively, from these bases.

This leaves lateral nasalization as the only plausible explanation.

The phenomenon is not peculiar to Tamil, either. In Kannada, we have «agundale», which is presumably cognate to Tamil «akal» and the suffix that forms «viṭu-talai». But how is such a sound change even motivated?

The Index Diachronica lists several sources that attest a sound change *‹l› > ‹n›. For example, we have Proto-Algonquian *‹l› > St. Francis and Kennebec River Abenaki ‹l ~ n›, wherein the nasal is only sometimes conditioned by a nearby nasal. However, my knowledge of Algonquian linguistics is too limited to evaluate the specifics of this process.

A possibility is rhinoglottophilia, wherein glottal sounds may later become nasal (Matisoff, 1975). If a glottal sound conditioned the *‹l›, then it could nasalize and conceivably become *‹n›. On one hand, we could posit preglottalization or glottal release as a native feature of *‹l›, but this might result in widespread *‹l› > *‹n›, regardless of conditioning environment. Instead, we could say that there was a glottal component in those suffixes that triggered the change; the only glottal phoneme (perhaps somewhat controversially) known to Dravidian is *‹h›, so the dental past-tense suffix, for example, may have taken the form *‹-ht-›. This could explain the varying distribution of so-called “strong” geminated and “weak” singleton dental-past suffixes across Dravidian, where the gemination cannot be a result of the dental effective suffix.

Background

When Karl F. Graul first classified the Tamil verb in his 1855 work Outline of Tamil Grammar (p. 38), he must have done so considering the morphophonological rules he had passed down to him from indigenous grammarians. They had some explanatory power, so he had no reason to doubt them. Therewith, he reduced all inflection into seven verb classes—four ‘weak,’ one ‘middle,’ and two ‘strong.’ Considering all possible inflections in Tamil, the following likely resembles the data he had to work with:

His classification would’ve been based on the following rules:

  • Verbs in Class 1 take a «-t-» past and a «-v-» future.
  • Verbs in Class 2 take an «-nt-» past and a «-v-» future.
  • Verbs in Class 3 take an «-iṉ-» past and a «-v-» future.
  • Verbs in Class 4 double «C₂» to form the past and add «-v-» for the future.
  • Verbs in Class 5 take a «-t-» past and a «-p-» future.
  • Verbs in Class 6 take a «-tt-» past and a «-pp-» future.
  • Verbs in Class 7 take an «-nt-» past and a «-pp-» future.

Class 1 Assumptions

It’s assumed that a lateral followed by ‹t› becomes ‹ṉṯ› or ‹ṇṭ›.
