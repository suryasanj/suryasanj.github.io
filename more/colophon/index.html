Indian-language transcription

General rules

I follow iso 15919 with some key exceptions:
  1. All palatal consonants are marked with a caron, so ‹c› → ‹č›, ‹j› → ‹ǰ›, ‹ñ› → ‹ň›, and ‹ś› → ‹š›.
  2. As a consequence, ‹c› represents a coronal (non-palatal) affricate, or what was initially represented by ‹ĉ›.
  3. Its voiced equivalent is represented by ‹ȷ›.
  4. Retroflexes are represented by dots above or below the consonant. This rule only affects the retroflex sibilant affricates, which become ‹ċ› and ‹j›.
  5. The letter representing the retroflex approximant in modern Tamil and all equivalent letters in related scripts are shown with ‹ṛ›.
  6. Since ‹ṅ› would now indicate a retroflex nasal, the velar nasal is instead marked by ‹ŋ›.
  7. The second member of true diphthongs has a subposed inverted breve ‹◌̯›.
  8. Vocalic consonants have a subposed vertical line ‹◌̩› instead of a ring ‹◌̥›.
  9. Metaphonemes like the Tamil aytam and the Sanskrit visarga and anusvara are shown with breves below as such: ‹ḫ› and ‹m̮›.

Broad transcription and transliteration is marked with single guillemets ‹› (or single angle brackets) for phonemes and double guillements «» (or double angle brackets) for morphemes or running text. Narrow transcription is marked with brackets []. Definitions in the text of the webpage are marked in double quotes “”.

Old Tamil and Malayalam

  1. Alveolar ‹ṟ› is instead ‹ṯ›.
  2. Overshort-u ‹ɯ› and overshort-i ‹ı› are explicitly marked.

Tulu

  1. The digraph ‹sd› ~ ‹sṭ› is marked ‹ζ› except in a reproduction of a text.
  2. The vowels generally transcribed as ‹ụ› and ‹ε› are now ‹ɯ› and ‹æ›, respectively.

Literary Telugu

  1. The ardhanusvara ‹n̆› is now represented as a tilde before the previous vowel.
  2. Overshort-u ‹ɯ› and overshort-i ‹ı› are explicitly marked.
