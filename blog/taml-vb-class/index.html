Tamil Verb Classification

The Personal Website of Surya Sanjay

Note. The contributions of this article are not entirely mine. A large portion of what is newly described was suggested by Dr. David McAlpin in personal communication. Ex post facto, he is a co-author of this work.

T amil verbs are subject to diverse morphophonology, leading to the myriad inflectional suffixes that vary with verbal base structure. As such, many rule-based classification schemes have been devised, both for the learner and for the historical linguist. These schemes range ontologically from minimalist to maximalist. Minimalist schemes explain morphological diversity by reducing the number of underlying forms, grouping inflections with ad hoc phonological rules. Maximalist schemes are conservative, making little to no groupings. In synchronic analysis, the former tends to align, to some extent, with reconstructed suffixes in proto-stages. Acknowledging the various motives and ontologies in verbal classification, I construct a minimalist scheme informed by comparative data in Literary Tamil (lt).

Three competing minimalist schemes are noteworthy, described by Graul (?), Lisker (1951), and Agesthialingom (1971) (see Table §§§). Graul’s seven-class system groups verbs into ‘strong’ or p-futures and ‘weak’ or v-futures. The v-futures are further divided into t-pasts (i), nt-pasts (ii), i-pasts (iii), and geminate-plosive pasts (iv). The p-futures, similarly, are divided into t-pasts (v), tt-pasts (vi), and nt-pasts (vii). Strong verbs take a present tense in «-kki(ṉ)ṟ-» and weak verbs in «-ki(ṉ)ṟ-». Lisker introduces a geminant «-×-» which has no decided phonetic or semantic value, yet can be analyzed as a part of the base, producing ‹tt› from «×-t» and ‹pp› from «×-v». This reduces Graul’s classes to three. Agesthialingom adds a small, yet significant, modification: he proposes that the homorganic nasal-plosive pasts for lateral final verbs belong to the nt-past class.

From a reconstructive perspective, this is dissatisfactory. There is no explanation given as to why some lateral-final bases take nasal-plosive pasts («mīḷ, mīṇṭ-, mīḷv-» “return” dedr 4883) whereas others take geminate-plosive pasts («mīḷ, mīṭṭ-, mīṭp-» “liberate” d° 4883). The synchronic rule ‹v› $\rightarrow$ ‹p› $/\,\{$ ṉ, ṇ $\}\,\_$ remains phonologically unmotivated. It’s unclear why the nt-class is split amongst weak and strong verbs. Finally, there is no clear consensus as to whether Agesthialingom’s addition, simultaneously proposed by Kumaraswamy Raja, holds.

My solution resolves these four issues, while providing insight into some aspects of the reconstruction of verb morphology at the Proto-Peninsular Dravidian (ppedr) stage.

Lateral-final Bases

Sixteen verbs in the Madras Tamil Lexicon (mtl) are strong lateral-final bases (see Table §§§). Of these, seven seem to have a backformed final (‘radical’) liquid, either from an original plosive or nasal.

d° 1297 supports a final lateral for a0 exclusively in South Dravidian (sdr), whereas both South-Central (scdr) and Central Dravidian (cdr) support *«kaṯ» for ppedr. Given a possible past stem *«kaṯ-ṯ-» ($\ll$ *«kaṯ-t-»), sdr backformed a secondary base *«kal», analogizing to other verbs in this class that inherited a lateral radical. a4 and a15 may be combined with d° 3031 and 5459 on similar grounds. d° 3123 is compared to 3031 *«taṭ» “id.” by Emeneau and Burrow; the former only attests in Kannada, Tulu and Telugu aside from Tamil, suggesting a possible diffusive origin of Kannada to Tulu and Telugu. This would isolate radical backformation to sdr. d° 5421 only in sdr and Telugu, and, as such, the Telugu form may be a loan. d° 5459 *«wīṯ» “id.” attests in cdr and scdr and thus may be combined with 5421, representing a more conserved form.

d° 3800 attests both «nōl» and «nōṉ» in Tamil, as well as Kannada «nōn». The Kannada form has past in «nōnt-», whose internal reconstruction would be *«nōnṯṯ-», warranting «nōṟṟ-» in Tamil. As such, the radical lateral form in Tamil may have backformed from earlier *«nōn, nōn-ṯṯ-, nōn-pp-». a8 is placed within an entry that has a nasal radical, with reconstructed *«(n)aṇ». Kannada «naṇṭa» “friend,” implying underlying *«naṇ-tt-an», directly parallels Tamil «naṭṭār» “friends,” which must derived from this verb. A similar radical backformation must have occurred for this etymon, as well, since the final lateral attests only in Tamil. Similar is a12, excepting having a direct past tense stem cognate elsewhere in Dravidian. a3 has cognates in Tulu and Gondi with a nasal radical, and Malto’s «qēɣe» may support this. However, the presence of a lateral radical in Kolami and Naiki makes this less likely to be a sdr-specific backformation.

At the same time, a13 curiously has a homophonous weak counterpart in the same d° entry, meaning “return.” Although (9) has slightly different semantics, the second meaning given in mtl (“recover, bring back”) is conceivable as an effective of “return.” As an affective-effective distinction is maintained by the weak-strong contract in the future and the dichotomy in past suffixes, it is conceivable that the underlying suffixes must be separated, in contrast to previous schemes. This may have additionally applied with a1, a11, and a15, as they have both weak- and strong-stem allomorphs, suggestive of a potential archaic affective-effective distinction before later semantic leveling.

Similarly, a much larger class of effective, strong tt-past bases have affective, weak nt-past counterparts, e. g., «vaḷar, vaḷar-nt-, vaḷar-v-» “grow (intr.)” d° 5304 and «vaḷar, vaḷar-tt-, vaḷar-pp-» “grow (tr.)” d° 5304. In parallel, I propose that weak lateral-final bases’ underlying tense suffixes are composed of «-(n)t-» in the past and «-v-» in the future, whereas strong lateral-final bases take «-tt-» and «-pp-», respectively. Thus, the following rules are supported:

$$ \require{unicode} \left\{ \begin{aligned}\text{l, ṉ} \\ \text{ḷ, ṇ}\end{aligned} \right\} + \text{-tt-} \rightarrow \left\{ \begin{aligned}\text{ṟṟ} \\ \text{ṭṭ}\end{aligned} \right\} $$

$$ \require{unicode} \left\{ \begin{aligned}\text{l, ṉ} \\ \text{ḷ, ṇ}\end{aligned} \right\} + \text{-pp-} \rightarrow \left\{ \begin{aligned}\text{ṟp} \\ \text{ṭp}\end{aligned} \right\} $$
